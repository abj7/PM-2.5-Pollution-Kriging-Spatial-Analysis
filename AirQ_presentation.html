Motivation

  • Fine particulate matter (PM2.5) is a mixture of air pollutants that, at a high concentration level, has adverse effects on human health.

  • Monitoring stations are deployed all over the US to measure total PM2.5 concentration.

  • However monitor stations are sparse, many places are not in the monitoring network. Hence, other data sources are needed for predicting PM2.5.

  • Another data source used by EPA to provide PM2.5 exposure estimate is the Community Multiscale Air Quality (CMAQ) model.

  • In this workshop, we will develop statistical methods that predicts PM2.5 concentration level using numerical model outputs and other available covariates.

  • Below are some R codes to help you get started.

  • File mappred_df contains all variables in dat file except PM2.5.

Cross validation.

  • Cross-validation is a technique to evaluate predictive models by partitioning the original sample into a training set to train the model, and a test set to evaluate it.
  • The last column of the file dat is called folds, and there are 5 folds in total.
  • You will build a statistical method using any four folds, says fold = 1,2,3,4, then make prediction for fold 5, as a way to evaluate your statistical model.
  • Now you can brainstorm for ideas on modeling PM2.5, try some of you favorite statistcal models!
  • There are different ways to evaluate how well your prediction matches with the observations. Can you name a few?
